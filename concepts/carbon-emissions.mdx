When sills intrude into organic-rich sedimentary rocks, they drive the surrounding sediments to temperatures far above their normal burial gradient. Organic matter that might take millions of years to mature under background geothermal heat can reach gas-generation temperatures within thousands of years of a sill emplacement event. TuLIP captures this process by coupling the temperature field to two alternative kinetic carbon models — SILLi and Sillburp — that calculate how quickly total organic carbon (TOC) breaks down and how much CO₂ is released at every grid node and every time step.

## SILLi model

The SILLi model implements the Easy%Ro approach of Sweeney & Burnham (1990), while the code is a direct implementation of the original MATLAB version of SILLi (Iyer et al., 2018). It represents the thermal cracking of organic matter as 20 parallel first-order Arrhenius reactions, each with its own activation energy drawn from a discrete distribution (34–72 kcal/mol). The cumulative reaction progress _F_ is used to derive vitrinite reflectance (%Ro) and to track how much TOC remains.

You invoke the SILLi model through `emit.SILLi_emissions()`:

```python
RCO2, Rom, percRo, TOC, W = emit.SILLi_emissions(
    T_field,     # current temperature field, °C
    density,     # rock density field, kg/m³
    lithology,   # rock type array
    porosity,    # porosity array
    TOC_prev,    # TOC from the previous time step
    dt,          # time step, s
    TOCo=TOCo,   # initial TOC at t=0 (required after first call)
    W=W,         # reaction state array, shape (20, nrows, ncols)
)
```

Key outputs:

| Variable | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| `RCO2` | Volumetric CO₂ release rate (kg m⁻³ s⁻¹) at each grid node |
| `Rom` | Organic-matter loss rate (kg m⁻³ s⁻¹); used to compute the endothermic heat sink |
| `percRo` | Vitrinite reflectance field (%Ro); a proxy for thermal maturity |
| `TOC` | Updated total organic carbon field (wt fraction) |
| `W` | Updated 20-channel reaction state array; pass back in the next time step |

## sillburp model

The sillburp model (Jones et al., 2019) resolves organic matter into four distinct kerogen components, each with its own activation energy distribution and frequency factor:

| Component | Reactions | Mean Eₐ | Frequency factor (A) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Labile kerogen | 7 | 208 kJ/mol | 1.58×10¹³ s⁻¹ |
| Refractory kerogen | 21 | 279 kJ/mol | 1.83×10¹⁸ s⁻¹ |
| Vitrinite (inert) | 55 | 242 kJ/mol | 4×10¹⁰ s⁻¹ |
| Oil-channel cracking | 7 | 230 kJ/mol | 1×10¹³ s⁻¹ |

You first compute the activation energy distributions once with `emit.get_sillburp_reaction_energies()`, then pass the result to `emit.sillburp()` at every time step:

```python
reaction_energies = emit.get_sillburp_reaction_energies()

RCO2, Rom, progress_of_reactions, oil_production_rate, TOC, rate_of_reactions = emit.sillburp(
    T_field,
    TOC_prev,
    density,
    lithology,
    porosity,
    dt,
    reaction_energies,
    TOCo=TOCo,
    progress_of_reactions=progress_of_reactions,
    rate_of_reactions=rate_of_reactions,
)
```

The `progress_of_reactions` array has shape `(4, 55, nrows, ncols)` and tracks the fractional completion of every reaction channel at every grid node. `oil_production_rate` records the intermediate generation of liquid hydrocarbons before they crack to gas — useful for assessing petroleum generation alongside CO₂ budgets.

## Choosing a model

<Accordion title="SILLi vs. Sillburp: a detailed comparison">
  **SILLi (Easy%Ro)**

  - Single organic-matter pool; all organic material is treated as vitrinite-equivalent.
  - 20 parallel reactions; lower memory footprint and faster per-step execution.
  - Directly outputs vitrinite reflectance (%Ro), which is the most widely measured thermal maturity proxy in sedimentary basins and can be validated against borehole data.
  - A good choice when you have limited kerogen-type information or need rapid sensitivity tests across many flux/volume scenarios.

  **Sillburp**

  - Resolves labile, refractory, and inert kerogen fractions plus an oil-cracking channel.
  - 90 total reaction channels; more memory and compute per step.
  - Accounts for the fact that labile kerogen cracks at lower temperatures than refractory kerogen — important when sill contact aureoles are narrow or heating rates are very high.
  - Produces oil generation rates alongside CO₂, making it suitable for petroleum systems analysis in addition to climate forcing estimates.
  - Requires you to supply or assume a kerogen-type breakdown for your study area.

  If you are primarily interested in bulk CO₂ budgets and have borehole %Ro data for validation, use **SILLi**. If you need kerogen-resolved maturation histories or oil-generation estimates, use **Sillburp**.
</Accordion>

## Initial thermal state

Sedimentary organic matter begins maturing as soon as rocks are buried — long before any LIP event begins. If you skip this pre-conditioning step, your simulation will overestimate CO₂ emissions because the model treats all TOC as fresh and unmatured at time zero.

You correct for this by calling the appropriate initialisation function before `emplace_sills()`:

```python
# For the SILLi model:
current_time, tot_RCO2, props_array, \
    RCO2_silli, Rom_silli, percRo_silli, curr_TOC_silli, W_silli, T_field = \
    sc.get_silli_initial_thermogenic_state(
        props_array,
        dt=3.156e9,          # time step, s
        method="conv smooth",
        time=3.156e13,       # pre-emplacement duration (~1 Myr), s
    )

# For the Sillburp model:
current_time, tot_RCO2, props_array, \
    RCO2, Rom, progress_of_reactions, oil_production_rate, curr_TOC, rate_of_reactions, \
    sillburp_weights, T_field = \
    sc.get_sillburp_initial_thermogenic_state(
        props_array,
        dt=3.156e9,
        method="conv smooth",
        time=3.156e13,
    )
```

Both functions advance the temperature field and kinetics over the specified pre-emplacement `time`, then return the fully conditioned state arrays ready to pass straight into `emplace_sills()`.
